Research

The Rux Lab studies how developmental signaling programs build synovial joints and establish lifelong articular cartilage structure—and how disruption of these programs contributes to osteoarthritis.

Synovial joint formation

We investigate how joint boundaries, synovial tissues, and surface specializations emerge during development.

  • Joint interzone and boundary specification
  • Synovium and lining cell development
  • Lubrication and surface protection programs

Articular cartilage morphogenesis

We study how zonal architecture is established postnatally and maintained into adulthood.

  • Zonal patterning and maturation
  • Extracellular matrix assembly and remodeling
  • Mechanosensation and tissue organization

Osteoarthritis mechanisms

We test how altered developmental pathways predispose cartilage to degeneration in injury and aging.

  • Post-traumatic and age-related OA models
  • Early molecular and cellular changes
  • Candidate protective and disease-driving pathways

Key themes

Our work connects developmental biology to musculoskeletal disease by focusing on how signaling gradients and cell-state transitions shape cartilage form and function over time.

Developmental signaling in adult tissue

We ask how pathways best known for development are tuned (“not too high, not too low”) to maintain adult articular cartilage integrity.

Spatial organization

We map where signals act in the joint and how spatial patterning relates to zonal identity, matrix features, and mechanical load.

From mechanism to disease vulnerability

We test how perturbations in these programs change injury responses and long-term outcomes relevant to OA.
